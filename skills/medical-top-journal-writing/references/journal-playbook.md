## JAMA-oriented default stance

Use JAMA as the default template when the user asks for a "medical top journal" but does not name a target. This default is chosen because JAMA publishes explicit manuscript-type rules and article structures that are easy to operationalize.

Use this file for structure and emphasis. Use live journal instructions before the final formatting pass if the user names another journal.

## Article-type defaults

These defaults are based on JAMA's current instructions for authors and are stable enough to use as a starting frame, not as a substitute for final verification.

### Original Investigation

- Default length: 3000 words of text
- Tables and figures: no more than 5 total
- Requires: structured abstract, 3 Key Points, data sharing statement
- Expect: EQUATOR-aligned reporting

### Randomized clinical trial

- Treat as an Original Investigation
- Subtitle convention: "A Randomized Clinical Trial"
- Include: trial registration and ID, trial protocol, CONSORT checklist, flow diagram, data sharing statement

### Nonrandomized clinical trial

- Treat as an Original Investigation
- Subtitle convention: "A Nonrandomized Clinical Trial"
- Include: registration and ID when applicable, protocol, flow diagram, data sharing statement
- Use TREND-style reporting logic

### Observational study

- Cohort, case-control, and cross-sectional studies follow the Original Investigation envelope
- Use STROBE-style reporting logic

### Meta-analysis

- Treat as an Original Investigation in JAMA
- Subtitle should include "A Meta-analysis"
- Use PRISMA logic and quantify the evidence base

### Systematic review without meta-analysis

- Typical frame: 3000 words, 50-75 references, 5 or fewer tables or figures
- Include: structured abstract, 3 Key Points, PRISMA-style flow diagram as supplement, evidence-quality table
- Subtitle convention: "A Systematic Review"
- Note: some journals require presubmission inquiry

### Narrative review

- Typical frame: 2000-3500 words, 50-75 references, 5 or fewer tables or figures
- Uses a 3-part structured abstract
- In JAMA-family logic, narrative reviews emphasize clinical applicability and synthesis over exhaustive methods reporting

## Structured abstract patterns

Use the exact pattern that matches the manuscript type.

### Research abstract

Use:

- Importance
- Objective
- Design
- Setting
- Participants
- Intervention(s) or Exposure(s)
- Main Outcomes and Measures
- Results
- Conclusions and Relevance

Notes:

- JAMA indicates that accepted papers may combine Design, Setting, and Participants during editing, but the submitted manuscript can keep them separate.
- Include study years and follow-up duration where relevant.
- Keep conclusions tightly tied to reported results and avoid overgeneralization.

### Systematic review or special communication abstract

Use:

- Importance
- Objective
- Evidence Review
- Findings
- Conclusions and Relevance

### Narrative review abstract

Use:

- Importance
- Observations
- Conclusions and Relevance

## Key Points

For JAMA-style research and review manuscripts, write a 75-100 word structured synopsis with exactly three items:

- Question: one-sentence study question
- Findings: one-sentence primary result, with size or sample context when possible
- Meaning: one-sentence clinical or policy implication

Do not duplicate the abstract. Key Points are a tighter editorial synopsis.

## Writing moves that usually help

- Put the clinical problem and why it matters in the first 2 to 3 sentences.
- Name the study design explicitly and early.
- Keep numerators, denominators, time windows, and confidence intervals visible.
- Translate findings into clinical relevance in the Discussion and abstract, not into hype.
- For reviews, make the evidence hierarchy obvious and give quantitative detail instead of broad adjectives.

## When to verify live journal rules

Verify the journal website before finalizing when:

- the user names a non-JAMA target journal
- the article type is uncommon or invited
- word count, figure count, supplement, or presubmission-inquiry rules matter for submission
- the journal has specific AI, data sharing, or patient involvement requirements
